Research

Project I: Giant Eruptions in Massive Stars and their Effect on the Stellar Structure Giant eruptions (GE) in Luminous blue variables (LBVs) are years to decades-long episodes of enhanced mass loss from the outer layers of the star during which the star undergoes major changes in its physical and observed properties. We use the \textsc{mesa} stellar evolution code to model the evolution of a 70 M⊙ star that undergoes a GE. As a result star contracts and releases a substantial amount of gravitational energy. The star undergoes an initial ≃3 days of expansion followed by years of contraction. Due to the loss of gravitational energy, its luminosity drops about one order of magnitude. As the GE terminates, we let the star continue to evolve without any further mass loss and track its recovery as it regains its equilibrium by adjusting its internal structure. After ≃87 years it reaches a state very close to the one where the GE was first initiated. We suggest that at this point another GE or a cycle of GEs may occurs.

Project II: Accretion and Recovery in Giant Eruptions of Massive Stars
When a very massive star in a binary system undergoes a Giant Eruption (GE), the companion star can accrete part of the mass. Unlike close binaries that experience continuous accretion and are affected over a long period of time, in GE the accretion occurs during one or few short events and at very high rates. We use numerical simulations to analyze how the companion responds to accretion and how its structure and evolution are altered. We run a grid of massive stars with masses from $20~\rm M_{\odot}$ to $60~\rm M_{\odot}$, and accretion rates from $\rm 10^{-4}$ to $\rm 0.1 ~M_{\odot}~\rm yr^{-1}$, over a duration of $20$ yrs. For accretion rates $\rm \lesssim 0.01 ~M_{\odot}~\rm yr^{-1}$ the star remains on the hotter side of the HR diagram with a minor increase in luminosity without expanding, as the accretion timescale exceeds the thermal time scale by a larger factor. Mass loss through stellar winds leads to a minor drop in luminosity shortly after the accretion phase as the star enters the recovery phase. For $\rm \gtrsim 0.01 ~M_{\odot}~\rm yr^{-1}$ the companion star experiences a sudden increase in luminosity by about one order of magnitude, inflates, and cools.

Project III: High Power Accretion in Massive Binary Systems and Impact of Metallicity
During a giant eruption of a very massive star in the binary system, the companion star can accrete a large amount of mass that changes its properties and potentially its subsequent evolution. The effect depends on the companion mass, metallicity, the amount of mass it accreted, orbital parameters and other parameters. We simulate individual companion stars assuming they undergo such accretion events. We study the envelope properties of 20 and 30 $\rm M_\odot$ single massive stars at different matallicities ($Z= 0.02$, $Z=0.008$ and $Z=0.004$) during accretion at different rates, from $\rm 10^{-5}$ to $\rm 10^{-2}~M_\odot\,yr^{-1}$. We find that both stars exhibit distinct behavior for low and high accretion rates. For the lower accretion rates, the star remains hot, while at higher accretion rates, it becomes cooler and inflates. This behavior is observed in both stars but occurs at different accretion rates. We investigated the effect of metallicity on accretion luminosity. We found that higher metallicity stars exhibited greater variations in accretion luminosity for the same accretion rate and stellar mass compared to lower metallicity stars. While higher metallicity typically expands the stellar envelope, suggesting smaller variations in luminosity at Galactic metallicity compared to the LMC and SMC, our results show the opposite. The change in accretion luminosity during the accretion phase indicates a potential dependence of accretion luminosity on metallicity.

Project IV: Effect of gravity darkening and oblate factor in rapidly rotating massive stars
With collaboration: Prof. Michel Cure, and Prof. Ignacio Araya Our study investigates the transition region where the fast solution of radiation-driven winds in a massive rotating star switches to the Omega-slow solution. This is applied to the most rapidly rotating stars among them, especially when the rotational speed is higher than 75 of the critical rotation speed. Dense and slow winds characterize Omega-slow solution. To explore the transition for various equatorial models of B-type stars, we are focusing on the co-existence interval where both solution simultaneously exits and the transition point where fast solutions switch to Omega-slow solutions. Using both time-steady and time-dependent numerical codes, we consider the presence of gravity darkening and distortion of the star’s shape due to high rotational speed.

Schools

MESA Summer School 2023
I attended the MESA Summer School on the stellar evolution code MESA, where I gained in-depth knowledge of the code and its applications. During the program, I had the opportunity to interact with the developers of MESA and explore fundamental astrophysical concepts through numerical simulations. The course covered a wide range of topics, including the structure and evolution of red giants, asteroseismology of early-type stars, Cepheids and nuclear reactions, stellar internal dynamics, and the evolution of massive binary systems

Stellar Winds in Extreme Massive Stars
I attended the POEMS workshop in 2023 in the Czech Republic, where we focused on stellar winds and the analysis of observational data to generate spectra. During the workshop, I gained hands-on experience with spectral analysis techniques and learned to use several advanced codes, including CMFGEN, HYDWIND, APPEAL, and SynCpec, to study the properties of massive stars through spectroscopy.
